# Assessment Quality Analysis
**Date:** 2026-03-01
**Analyzed:** 23 core assessments (210 items total)
**Scope:** Item quality, psychometric properties, construct validity, engagement risk

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Aura assessments form a well-structured tiered system with generally solid psychometric fundamentals, but contain opportunities for improvement in construct clarity, scale appropriateness, and sensitivity management.

**Key Strengths:**
- Big Five tests show strong reverse-keying (5 items each, preventing acquiescence bias)
- Starter Pack provides good behavioral breadth with mixed polarity
- Reasoning tests show appropriate difficulty progression
- Clear tier system with logical unlock flow

**Key Concerns:**
- ADHD screen is too short (6 items) and lacks temporal specificity
- Shadow Self items are all positive-keyed; risks acquiescence bias
- Some items conflate multiple constructs (e.g., Risk Tolerance mixes ethical/physical/social)
- Attachment scale could feel invasive without framing
- Limited diversity in some scales (e.g., Chronotype is purely agreement-based)

## DETAILED ANALYSIS BY TEST

### TIER 0: GATEWAY

#### **quick-profile** (10 items, likeme + gateway scales)

**Item Quality: B+**
- **Strengths:**
  - Conversational tone ("quiet time to recharge" vs clinical phrasing)
  - Clear behavioral referents (party behavior, routine consistency)
  - Items 2 & 3 effectively distinguish C and A dimensions

- **Issues:**
  - Item phrasing is sometimes awkward: "creature of habit — same routines, same accounts, same spots" conflates behavioral consistency with social/digital footprint disclosure
  - "The people closest to me really know me" (item 1) is ambiguous—could mean intimacy OR ease of reading
  - The branching structure means different users see different questions—unclear if this is by design or oversight

**Balance: B**
- No reverse-keyed items (all positively framed)
- Gateway branching adds complexity—unclear if this improves measurement or just engagement

**Construct Validity: C+**
- Items map to Big Five dimensions (E, A, C, O, N) loosely
- Item 0 (energy) conflates Extraversion with introvert/ambivert/extrovert categories—good for engagement, less precise psychometrically
- Missing Openness entirely in core 3 items; handled only in branches

**Face Validity: A-**
- Users would understand what's being measured
- "Creature of habit" and "people closest to me" feel personal and relevant
- Might feel patronizing to those who don't fit archetypes (e.g., highly conscientious extroverts)

**Item Count: A**
- 10 items for a gateway is appropriate; under 2 minutes as advertised

**Scale Appropriateness: B-**
- Mixing likeme and gateway scales is confusing—consider unifying
- Gateway options (0: Quiet time, 1: Depends, 2: Going out) are good for branching but less precise than Likert for scoring

**Sensitivity: A**
- Neutral territory; no pathologizing language
- However, "Definitely—they get me" vs "I keep most people at a distance" could make socially anxious users uncomfortable

**Missing Constructs:**
- No pure Openness item in core set (creativity, risk, novelty)
- Mood stability (N) only touched in branches

**Recommendation:**
- Unify scale presentation (all gateway or all likeme)
- Add one pure Openness item to core 3
- Clarify: does branching improve accuracy or just feel fun? If the latter, consider dropping it for simplicity

### TIER 1: STARTER PACK (5 modules, 40 items total)

#### **starter-personality** (9 items, likeme)

**Item Quality: B**
- **Strengths:**
  - Clear behavioral anchors: "I am the first person to introduce myself" (concrete, not vague)
  - Item 2 is sophisticated: "I apologize first after an argument, even if I am not wrong" captures trait A well
  - Item 4 & 5 good reverse pairs for C and E

- **Issues:**
  - Item 7 is a trade-off ("I choose new experiences over sticking to familiar routines")—unclear how to score this without additional context
  - Item 3 ("physically tense") conflates body sensation with emotional state—psychologically sound but assumes users can recognize this link
  - Item 8 mirrors item 3 (both N+); wastes bandwidth given 9-item limit

**Balance: B-**
- 5 positive, 3 reverse, 1 trade-off
- Good ratio, but trade-off item lacks a reverse pair

**Construct Validity: A-**
- Maps clearly to Big Five (E, C, A, N, O)
- Item 2 is excellent multi-trait item (captures agreeableness via warmth)
- Item 7 is problematic: "new experiences" signals Openness, but "stick to familiar" signals Conscientiousness—could be two items

**Face Validity: A**
- Highly relatable; users get why each item matters
- No clinical language; accessible tone

**Item Count: B**
- 9 items is short but reasonable for starter module
- E & C get 2 items each; O gets 1 (underrepresented)

**Scale Appropriateness: A**
- Likeme scale fits perfectly for personality self-assessment

**Sensitivity: A**
- Safe territory; no pathologizing

**Missing Constructs:**
- Openness under-measured (only 1 item)
- No facets within Big Five (e.g., E: enthusiasm vs. assertiveness)
- N should have more cognitive-worry items, not just body-sensing

**Recommendation:**
- Split item 7 into two: one for Openness (new experiences), one for Conscientiousness (routine)
- Reduce items 3 & 8 overlap—keep one N item that focuses on rumination or worry
- Add second Openness item

#### **adhd (ADHD Screen)** 6 items, frequency scale

**Item Quality: B-**

- **Strengths:**
  - Items are based on DSM-5 criteria for ADHD, validated instrument basis
  - Behavioral specificity: "I have trouble wrapping up final details," "I have difficulty getting organized"

- **Issues:**
  - Test is extremely short (6 items) for a clinical screening tool; typically ADHD screens have 12-18 items
  - No temporal specificity: "I have difficulty getting organized for tasks" — doesn't specify how recently or consistently
  - Items conflate executive dysfunction, hyperactivity, and inattention without clear subscale structure
  - Item 4 & 5 measure hyperactivity-impulsivity facet (fidget, driven by motor); Items 0-3 measure inattention/executive function — no internal structure

**Balance: B**
- All 6 items are positive-keyed (problems/symptoms); no reverse items
- However, this is appropriate for screening (not trait measurement)—all items should indicate risk, not balance

**Construct Validity: C+**
- Items map to ADHD symptoms, but with confounding:
  - Items 0-3: executive function / inattention (wrapping up, organizing, remembering, procrastinating)
  - Items 4-5: hyperactivity (fidgeting, restlessness)
  - No clear subscale; unclear if this screens for combined, inattentive, or hyperactive presentation
- Item 2 ("I have problems remembering appointments") could indicate memory impairment, not ADHD
- Item 3 ("I avoid or delay starting tasks") conflates procrastination (could be anxiety, depression, low motivation) with ADHD

**Face Validity: B-**
- Users would understand these are ADHD symptoms
- However, some items are so common in general population that users might feel over-pathologized
- "I have trouble wrapping up final details" — this is normal; ADHD would be "almost always"

**Item Count: F**
- 6 items is dangerously short for clinical screening
- Standard ADHD screening tools (ASRS, CPT, Conners) have 12-18+ items
- Insufficient to reliably distinguish ADHD from other conditions (anxiety, depression, procrastination)
- Likely generates false positives and false negatives

**Scale Appropriateness: A**
- Frequency scale (Never, Rarely, Sometimes, Often, Always) is appropriate for symptom screening
- Better than likeme scale for this content

**Sensitivity: B-**
- ADHD screening touches on clinical territory; questions are appropriate but not preceded by framing
- Users might feel pathologized ("Do I have ADHD?")
- Recommend: add context like "These questions explore how you process tasks and attention—not a diagnosis."

**Missing Constructs:**
- No emotional dysregulation items (ADHD often involves emotion dyscontrol)
- No time blindness / time perception items (core ADHD symptom)
- No hyperfocus items (flip side of inattention)
- Hyperactivity only covered briefly (2 items); should be at least 3-4 for balance

**Recommendation: SIGNIFICANT REVISION NEEDED**
- Expand to at least 12 items minimum
- Add subscales: Inattention (4-5 items), Hyperactivity (3-4 items), Executive function (3-4 items)
- Add items on time perception, emotional dysregulation
- Add clinical framing: "ADHD affects how people focus, organize, and manage time. These questions explore that; they're not a diagnosis."
- Consider partnering with validated ADHD screening tool (ASRS or Conners) rather than inventing from scratch
- Current test is too short and risky for accurate screening

#### **cognitive (Cognitive Style)** 10 items, agreement scale

**Item Quality: B+**

- **Strengths:**
  - Items are specific and behavioral: "I notice things others miss—small sounds, subtle changes"
  - Item 8 ("I enjoy organizing info into categories and systems") is excellent indicator of autistic cognitive style
  - Item 9 ("Learning rules and patterns is deeply satisfying") similarly strong

- **Issues:**
  - Items are heavily skewed toward autistic/neurodivergent traits without explicitly naming this
  - Item 4 ("I take things literally, missing the subtext") is somewhat crude; assumes users recognize they miss subtext
  - Item 5 & 6 conflate social understanding with literal interpretation; unclear if measuring same construct

**Balance: C**
- All 10 items are positive-keyed (endorsing detail focus, literal thinking, pattern recognition, systems)
- No reverse items; risks acquiescence bias
- Items 0-7 measure "autistic-like" cognitive traits
- Items 8-9 measure satisfaction with systems/patterns
- Asymmetric structure

**Construct Validity: B**
- Items map to broader construct of "cognitive style" but with specific slant toward autism/neurodivergence
- Items 0-7: detail focus, literal interpretation, sensory sensitivity, difficulty with social pragmatics
- Items 8-9: systems orientation, pattern satisfaction
- These could be two separate constructs: "perceptual detail" + "systems thinking"
- Item 7 ("Movie characters' decisions often puzzle me") is weak proxy for social cognition

**Face Validity: B**
- Users would understand questions, but might feel like being diagnosed for autism
- Framing as "cognitive style" vs. "autism screening" matters enormously

**Item Count: A-**
- 10 items is reasonable for cognitive style dimension
- Could be expanded if splitting into subscales

**Scale Appropriateness: A**
- Agreement scale appropriate for trait measurement

**Sensitivity: C**
- **RED FLAG:** This test essentially screens for autistic traits without naming it
- Items like "I take things literally, missing the subtext" could feel pathologizing to autistic users or those with social communication differences
- Items could be perceived as: "Are you weird?" vs. "What's your cognitive processing style?"
- Risk: users take this test thinking it's neutral cognitive assessment, then get feedback that pathologizes divergent thinking
- No context/framing provided

**Missing Constructs:**
- No measure of divergent vs. convergent thinking
- No creative problem-solving
- No intuitive vs. analytical thinking
- Systems thinking only measured by 2 items (8-9)

**Recommendation: MODERATE REVISION NEEDED**
- Add context/framing: "Cognitive style describes how you process information—no style is better or worse."
- Consider renaming from "Cognitive Style" to more specific dimension (e.g., "Detail Orientation" or "Sensory Processing")
- OR add companion items that measure non-autistic cognitive traits (creativity, intuition, big-picture thinking) to balance out
- Add 2-3 reverse items: "I naturally see the big picture before details," "I tend to interpret things contextually rather than literally"
- Be transparent in feedback: results describe cognitive patterns, not "diagnoses" or "disorders"

#### **attachment (Attachment Style)** 12 items, agreement scale

**Item Quality: B**

- **Strengths:**
  - Items map to established attachment theory (Bowlby, Ainsworth)
  - Good behavioral specificity: "When I don't hear from someone, I assume something is wrong"
  - Item 5 ("I push for closeness in ways that feel desperate after") is sophisticated, self-aware

- **Issues:**
  - Items are all self-report and depend on user honesty about vulnerabilities
  - Item 0 ("When I don't hear from someone, I assume something is wrong") is extreme; normal people briefly wonder too
  - Item 3 ("I need more reassurance than most that I'm valued") is comparative; unclear if user knows what "most" people need
  - Avoidant items (6-11) are quite direct; "I'm most comfortable not depending on anyone" could trigger defensiveness

**Balance: B-**
- Items 0-5: anxious attachment (6 items)
- Items 6-11: avoidant attachment (6 items)
- No items measuring secure attachment (absence of anxiety + avoidance)
- All items are positive-keyed within their dimension (endorsing anxious or avoidant traits)
- No reverse items within anxious subscale; risks acquiescence bias within that dimension

**Construct Validity: A-**
- Anxious items (0-5) clearly measure: worry about relationship security, seeking reassurance, fear of abandonment, desperate pursuit
- Avoidant items (6-11) clearly measure: discomfort with dependence, emotional guardedness, distance
- These are two orthogonal dimensions (not opposite ends of one scale), which is correct for attachment theory
- Item 11 ("I've been told I'm hard to read") is behavioral/external feedback rather than self-report; weakest item

**Face Validity: B-**

### 2. **Construct Clarity**
- **Problem:** Some tests mix constructs or are unclear about what they measure
  - Risk Tolerance = 4 separate constructs (financial, physical, social, ethical)
  - Integrity = 2 constructs (honesty, status-seeking) but labeled as one
  - Cognitive Style = autism screening without naming it
  - Connection = attachment + anxiety + trust, mixed in same scale

**Recommendation:** Either split unclear tests into subscales or explicitly label what subscales mean.

### 3. **Item Count & Reliability**
- Tests with <8 items: ADHD (6), Integrity (6), Shadow-M (6), Shadow-N (6), Shadow-P (6), Chronotype (6)
- For threshold-level reliability (α ≥ 0.60), need minimum 3-4 items per dimension
- But for publication-quality (α ≥ 0.70), 6 items is borderline; 8+ is safer
- **Problem:** 6 shadow tests are too short to be reliable

**Recommendation:** Expand short tests to 8-10 items minimum, or accept lower reliability estimates in scoring.

### 4. **Sensitivity & Framing**
- **Clinical/Vulnerable Content:**
  - ADHD (neurodevelopmental screening)
  - Attachment (relationship vulnerabilities)
  - Cognitive (autism traits)
  - Shadow Self (dark personality traits)

- **Missing:** Framing/context for these sensitive tests
  - No statement like "These questions are for self-exploration, not diagnosis"
  - No normalization (e.g., "Most people have some anxiety or avoidance")
  - No validation of alternative results (e.g., "Secure attachment is about low anxiety + low avoidance")

**Recommendation:** Add contextual framing for any test measuring clinical/dark content. Examples:
- ADHD: "These explore how you process tasks and manage attention—not a diagnosis."
- Attachment: "Attachment describes your relationship patterns; all styles are normal and can adapt."
- Shadow Self: "These explore personality tendencies; high scores aren't 'bad'—they reflect different ways of thinking about relationships."

---

### 5. **Scale Appropriateness**
Most tests use likeme or agreement 5-point scales, which is appropriate. Some suggestions:
- **Risk:** Likelihood scale is good; all items are concrete scenarios
- **ADHD:** Frequency scale is appropriate (Never, Rarely, Sometimes, Often, Always)
- **Reasoning:** Multiple choice is appropriate for objective scoring
- **quick-profile:** Gateway scale works for branching, but consistency with Starter Pack scale would help

---

### 6. **Missing Constructs Across System**
- **Values / Ethics:** No test explicitly measures values (partially in Integrity, Risk)
- **Meaning-Making:** No test measures spirituality, purpose, existential concerns
- **Resilience / Coping:** No explicit resilience measure
- **Social Dominance:** Implicit in some tests, not explicit
- **Creativity:** No standalone creativity test (some in Openness)
- **Emotional Intelligence:** No explicit test (some in Big Five A, N)
- **Authenticity / Self-Alignment:** No measure of living according to values

**Recommendation:** Consider whether these gaps matter for Aura's purpose. If person-centered insight is goal, some of these missing constructs might be valuable.

## CONCLUSION

Aura's assessment system is well-structured with a logical tier progression. The Big Five suite is gold-standard; Starter Pack is engaging and behaviorally grounded; Reasoning tests show good difficulty progression. However, significant quality variance exists:

- **Tier 0 & 1:** Mostly solid (B+ to A range)
- **Tier 2:** Highly variable (C+ to A range), with critical gaps in ADHD, Shadow Self, and Cognitive tests

**Overall assessment:** B (good framework, meaningful revisions needed for full implementation)

The system prioritizes engagement and accessibility, which is appropriate for a self-exploration app. However, if Aura plans to make clinical or developmental recommendations based on these tests (e.g., "you might have ADHD"), significantly higher psychometric standards would be needed.

Recommend reviewing and revising in this order:
1. ADHD (safety concern: too short for clinical screening)
2. Shadow Self tests (acquiescence bias + unclear ethical intent)
3. Cognitive test (framing + reverse keying)
4. Remaining Full Assessments (as detailed above)
