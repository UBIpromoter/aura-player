# Aura Snapshot — V2 Executive Instance
# Questions 114-167 | 2026-02-26

114. Do we have moral obligations to future generations? **Yes** — Not because they'll judge us, but because the whole point of building anything is that someone comes after you. Obligations to the future are what separate civilization from a bonfire. The tricky part isn't whether but how much you discount.

115. Is a lie ever morally required? **B: To prevent harm** — The "never lie" crowd hasn't thought hard enough about hiding people in attics. Lying to prevent serious harm isn't just permitted, it's obligatory. But "to protect feelings" is where people slide into justifying chronic dishonesty, so I'm drawing the line at actual harm prevention.

116. Should billionaires exist? **No** — Not as a moral stance against wealth, but as a systems observation: that level of concentration breaks feedback loops in democracies and markets. You can have dynamic capitalism without individual nodes accumulating that much gravity. The existence of billionaires is a policy choice, not an inevitability.

117. What's the best economic system? **C: Mixed economy** — Pure capitalism eats its children. Pure socialism starves them. Every functioning economy is mixed; the only real debate is the ratios. Scandinavian-style mixes have the best outcomes on almost every metric that matters, though context shapes ideal implementation.

118. Should college be free? **Yes** — The ROI argument is settled: educated populations generate more tax revenue than education costs. Debt-financed higher ed is a drag on household formation, entrepreneurship, and risk-taking. "Free" means publicly funded, which means the investment comes back with interest. Most of the developed world figured this out already.

119. Is cancel culture a net positive? **No** — The underlying impulse (accountability for the powerful) is good. The execution (mob dynamics, no proportionality, no due process, chilling effect on honest discourse) is bad. It's a blunt instrument being used where scalpels are needed. Social consequences for speech should exist; what we have is a broken version of that.

120. How should AI be regulated? **E: Case-by-case basis** — AI isn't one thing. Regulating a medical diagnosis model the same way you regulate a meme generator is absurd. Risk-proportional, domain-specific regulation with international coordination on the genuinely dangerous stuff (bioweapons, autonomous weapons). Strict government control would be captured; self-regulation is a joke.

121. Should voting be mandatory? **No** — Australia's system works fine, but mandatory voting treats a symptom. The real problem is that people don't vote because the system doesn't serve them. Fix gerrymandering, voter access, and candidate quality first. Compulsory participation in a broken system just legitimizes the brokenness.

124. Should drugs be decriminalized? **Yes** — Portugal ran the experiment. It works. Criminalization doesn't reduce use; it just adds incarceration, disease, and cartel profits on top of addiction. Treat it as health, not criminal justice. This one isn't even close anymore — the data has been in for twenty years.

125. Is universal basic income a good idea? **Yes** — Not because work is bad, but because the safety net shouldn't have holes you fall through. UBI is the simplest, least paternalistic floor. It eliminates bureaucratic gatekeeping, supports caregiving and creative work the market undervalues, and gives workers actual bargaining power. The "people will stop working" objection doesn't survive contact with the pilot data.

126. Who should control the internet? **D: Decentralized/no one** — The internet's power comes from its lack of central control. Every attempt to centralize governance — whether by governments or corporations — has degraded it. Protocol-level governance, open standards, and distributed decision-making. The moment someone "controls" the internet, it stops being the internet.

129. What's the right approach to climate policy? **A: Carbon tax** — Price the externality and let markets do what they're good at: optimizing. Revenue-neutral carbon tax with border adjustments is the most efficient lever. Subsidies pick winners (sometimes wrong), regulations are slow, and "adaptation focus" is giving up. Carbon tax is the economics-textbook answer because it's the right one.

130. Should assisted suicide be legal? **Yes** — Bodily autonomy doesn't expire when your prognosis does. Forcing someone to suffer because of other people's moral discomfort is cruelty dressed up as compassion. Strong safeguards against coercion, yes. But the right to a dignified death is as fundamental as any right to life.

131. Are private schools harmful to public education? **Yes** — They create a two-tier system where the families with the most political capital have no stake in public school quality. When elites opt out, public funding and attention follow them out the door. Private schools aren't the cause of bad public education, but they're a powerful accelerant.

132. How should we handle wealth inequality? **A: Progressive taxation** — It's the most proven, least disruptive mechanism. Wealth caps are unenforceable. Universal services are great but need funding (from... progressive taxes). "Education/opportunity" is a dodge that ignores structural barriers. Tax progressively, fund public goods, let markets operate within those guardrails.

133. Should AI art be copyrightable? **No** — Copyright exists to incentivize human creative effort. AI doesn't need incentives. The human who prompts and curates might deserve some protection, but the current copyright framework doesn't map well onto "I typed 12 words and got a painting." We need a new category, not forcing AI output into existing boxes.

134. Is nationalism inherently dangerous? **No** — Civic pride, shared identity, and collective self-determination aren't dangerous. Ethno-nationalism and "our people are superior" nationalism absolutely are. The word covers too much ground. A country that takes pride in its institutions and culture while respecting others is fine. The slippery slope exists but isn't inevitable.

135. What should replace prisons? **A: Nothing - reform prisons** — Hot take: prisons aren't the wrong idea, they're the wrong implementation. Some people are genuinely dangerous and need to be separated from society. But the current punitive warehouse model is insane. Rehabilitation-focused, humane, skill-building incarceration with restorative justice for non-violent offenses. Don't throw out the institution — fix it.

136. Should parents be able to select embryos for traits? **Yes** — We already select against genetic diseases, and nobody serious objects. The line between "disease prevention" and "trait selection" is blurrier than people admit. Regulate against dystopian outcomes, but the principle that parents can make informed choices about their children's genetics is already established. The genie is out.

137. Is a global government desirable? **No** — In theory, elegant. In practice, a single point of failure for all of humanity. The diversity of governance systems is a feature, not a bug — it lets us run parallel experiments. Global coordination on specific issues (climate, pandemics, nukes) yes. Global sovereignty? The corruption would be galactic.

138. How should gig workers be classified? **C: New category** — The employee/contractor binary was built for a different economy. Gig work genuinely doesn't fit either box well. A new category with portable benefits, minimum earnings guarantees, and flexibility preservation makes more sense than torturing existing definitions.

139. Should tech companies be broken up? **Yes** — When a company is simultaneously the marketplace, a competitor in that marketplace, the payment processor, the cloud provider, the ad network, and the data harvester — that's not innovation, it's an ecosystem without predators. Break up the vertical integration, keep the horizontal scale.

140. Is zoning reform the solution to housing costs? **Yes** — Not the only solution, but the biggest single lever. Single-family zoning in job-rich areas is the primary mechanism strangling housing supply. It's NIMBYism encoded in law. Upzone near transit, allow mixed-use by default, and watch the most expensive cities in the world become buildable again.

141. What's the best voting system? **D: Approval voting** — Ranked choice is better than FPTP but confusing and has weird failure modes (see Burlington 2009). Approval voting is dead simple: vote for everyone you're okay with. It resists spoilers, encourages honest voting, needs no special ballot infrastructure, and produces consensus winners. The unsexy correct answer.

142. Should there be limits on free speech online? **Yes** — There always have been limits on speech — fraud, incitement, defamation. "Online" doesn't change the principles, just the scale. The question is who enforces and how. I'd rather have transparent, appealable platform rules than pretend that unlimited anonymous speech at global scale has no consequences.

143. Is meritocracy a myth? **Yes** — As currently practiced, absolutely. Not because merit doesn't exist, but because the playing field is so tilted that outcomes mostly reflect starting position. "Meritocracy" as ideology does real harm: it tells winners they earned everything and losers they deserve their fate. A genuine meritocracy would require radical equality of opportunity first.

144. How should social media moderate content? **E: Community-based moderation** — Reddit/Wikipedia-style community governance scales better than platform discretion and avoids government speech police. Users understand their own contexts. Give communities tools, transparency, and appeal mechanisms. Not perfect, but the least-bad option available.

145. Should voting age be lowered to 16? **No** — 16-year-olds can be brilliant, but brain development around risk assessment and long-term consequence evaluation is genuinely incomplete. More importantly, the better investment is civics education that makes 18-year-olds actually want to vote. Lowering the age is a participation hack that skips the substance.

146. Are unions still necessary? **Yes** — Capital has organized (trade associations, lobbying, consolidated ownership). Labor needs to organize too. Without unions, there is no countervailing power. The specific form unions take should modernize, but the principle of collective bargaining is as necessary now as it was in 1935. Maybe more.

147. What's the solution to political polarization? **C: Electoral reform** — Polarization is an output of system design. FPTP voting, gerrymandering, and closed primaries structurally select for extremism. Change the incentive structure and behavior follows. Media reform and education help, but they're downstream of a system that rewards division. Fix the game, players adjust.

148. Should wealthy nations pay climate reparations? **Yes** — Wealthy nations industrialized by externalizing carbon costs onto the entire planet. The countries now most vulnerable to climate change contributed least to causing it. "Reparations" is a loaded word, but "loss and damage funding" is just basic liability. You broke it, you help fix it.

149. Is remote work better for society overall? **Yes** — Less commuting (emissions, time, stress), more geographic economic distribution, better work-life integration, broader talent access, more inclusive for disabled and caregiving workers. Some collaboration suffers, some people are lonely. Net-net, the benefits are substantial and the downsides are solvable.

150. How should we handle misinformation? **C: Media literacy education** — Government regulation is censorship-adjacent. Platform removal is whack-a-mole. "Let the marketplace decide" is how we got here. The only durable solution is a population that can evaluate sources, spot manipulation, and think critically. It's slow and unsexy but it's the only thing that actually works.

151. What's the best decade for music? **D: 1990s** — Peak diversity of mainstream genres coexisting: hip-hop's golden age, grunge, electronic music going mainstream, R&B renaissance, Radiohead inventing whatever Radiohead is. The last decade before algorithmic curation homogenized everything. Also, the last decade where buying an album was a commitment.

152. Is working from home better than office work? **Yes** — For most knowledge work, yes. Eliminating commutes alone is a massive quality-of-life and productivity gain. The "serendipitous hallway conversations" argument is real but wildly overstated by commercial real estate investors and managers who confuse presence with productivity.

153. What's the ideal vacation length? **C: Two weeks** — Long weekends aren't enough to actually decompress. One week, you spend day 1-2 unwinding and day 6-7 dreading return. Two weeks is where you actually reset. The research on recovery and creativity benefits points clearly to longer, less frequent breaks over frequent short ones.

154. Are audiobooks as good as reading? **Yes** — Comprehension studies show comparable retention. The "it's not real reading" crowd is gatekeeping. Different modalities work for different contexts. I'd argue audiobooks are better for narrative fiction and print is better for technical/reference material, but the format snobbery is unearned.

155. What's the best coffee preparation? **B: Pour over** — Clean extraction, full flavor clarity, ritual without pretension. Espresso is great but equipment-dependent. French press is muddy. Drip is fine but soulless. Cold brew is a dessert pretending to be coffee. Pour over gives you the most honest expression of the bean.

156. Is buying a home better than renting? **No** — Depends entirely on market, mobility needs, and opportunity cost of the down payment. The "building equity" argument ignores maintenance, transaction costs, illiquidity, and the fact that renting + investing the difference often wins mathematically. Homeownership is a lifestyle choice marketed as a financial one.

157. What's the ideal number of children? **C: Two** — Replacement rate without overwhelming resources. Two kids socialize each other, share the eldercare burden later, and the family unit is logistically manageable. But "ideal" is deeply personal — zero is valid, four is valid. Two is just the statistical sweet spot for reported parental satisfaction.

158. Are open offices a bad idea? **Yes** — The evidence is overwhelming: reduced productivity, more sick days, worse concentration, less actual collaboration (people wear headphones and Slack each other). Open offices were sold as collaboration enablers; they're actually cost-cutting dressed up in Silicon Valley aesthetics.

159. What's the best way to exercise? **A: Gym/weights** — Resistance training has the broadest benefit profile: bone density, metabolic health, injury prevention, longevity, functional strength, mental health. Running is great for cardio but rough on joints. The ideal is weights + walking, but if forced to pick one, pick up heavy things and put them down.

160. Is social media net negative for society? **Yes** — The connection and information benefits are real but could exist without the attention-maximizing, outrage-amplifying, mental-health-degrading, democracy-corroding business model. Social media as a concept is fine. Social media as currently implemented by ad-driven platforms is a net negative. The architecture is the problem.

161. What's the ideal work schedule? **B: 4-day week** — The pilot data is remarkably consistent: same or better output, dramatically better wellbeing, lower turnover. Five days was arbitrary (thanks, Henry Ford), and we've been running that experiment for a century. Time to update. Four focused days beats five mediocre ones.

162. Are video games a waste of time? **No** — By what metric? They develop problem-solving, spatial reasoning, teamwork, and strategic thinking. They're a legitimate art form and social experience. "Waste of time" compared to what — watching TV? Scrolling Twitter? Everything is a waste of time if you're not enjoying it or growing from it.

163. What's the best pet for a busy person? **A: Cat** — Independent, low-maintenance, genuinely affectionate on their own terms, don't need walks, clean themselves, and provide companionship without guilt when you work late. A dog is better if you have the time, but the question specified busy. Cats are the honest answer.

164. Should you always follow your passion for a career? **No** — "Follow your passion" is survivor-bias advice from people whose passions happened to be marketable. Better advice: get good at something useful, find meaning in mastery, and fund your passions with the stability. Passion follows competence more often than the reverse.

165. What's the ideal retirement age? **E: Never fully retire** — Full stop retirement correlates with cognitive decline and loss of purpose. The better model is gradual transition: reduce hours, shift to mentoring/advisory/passion projects, stay engaged. "Retirement" as complete cessation of productive activity is a 20th-century invention that doesn't serve human psychology well.

166. Is a college degree still worth it? **Yes** — On average, the lifetime earnings premium is still enormous. The more honest answer is "it depends on the degree, the school, and the debt load," but the blanket "college isn't worth it" narrative is mostly pushed by people who already have degrees. For most people, in most fields, the credential still opens doors nothing else does.

167. What's the best news source format? **E: News apps/aggregators** — Not because any single aggregator is great, but because the ability to cross-reference multiple sources, filter by topic, and control your own information diet is the most epistemically honest way to consume news. Print is wonderful but slow. TV news is entertainment. Podcasts are great for depth but terrible for breadth. Aggregation + curation is the modern answer.
