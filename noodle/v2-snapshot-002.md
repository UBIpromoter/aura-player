# Aura Snapshot — V2 Executive Instance
# Questions 63-113 | 2026-02-26

63. Will AI-generated content be majority of internet content by 2035? **Yes** — It already is by some metrics. SEO slop, synthetic images, chatbot outputs. By 2035 the question won't be "if" but "how do we find the human-made stuff." The floodgates opened in 2023 and nobody's closing them.

64. How will most people commute in major cities in 2040? **B: Public transit** — The boring answer is right. Cars don't scale, autonomous ride-share is still cars in traffic, e-bikes are weather-dependent, and remote work peaked. Cities that function in 2040 will have invested in transit. The ones that didn't will be traffic nightmares with Waymos.

65. Will any tech company reach $10 trillion market cap by 2035? **Yes** — Apple or Microsoft or whoever wins the AI platform war. $3.5T to $10T is roughly a 3x over 11 years. The S&P has historically done ~10% annually. A dominant AI company capturing massive value creation? 3x is aggressive but plausible, especially with currency inflation baked in.

66. Will lab-grown organs be standard medical practice by 2040? **No** — "Standard" is doing a lot of heavy lifting. We'll have some lab-grown organs in clinical use, sure. But standard medical practice means routine, widespread, insurance-covered. Biology is hard, regulation is slow, and scaling biotech manufacturing is a nightmare. 2040 is optimistic by a decade.

67. Which renewable will grow fastest 2025-2035? **A: Solar** — Solar's cost curve is a juggernaut. It's already the cheapest electricity source in most of the world and it's still getting cheaper. Nothing else has that kind of exponential deployment curve. Nuclear is great but takes 10+ years to build. Solar you can install on a roof next Tuesday.

68. Will physical cash be eliminated in any major economy by 2035? **No** — Sweden keeps threatening to and keeps not doing it. Cash serves too many legitimate purposes — privacy, resilience, inclusion. Even the most digital economies keep it around as a backstop. Reduced dramatically? Yes. Eliminated? No major economy will pull that trigger by 2035.

69. Will we discover definitive evidence of extraterrestrial life by 2050? **No** — I want to say yes so badly. But "definitive" is the killer word. We might find biosignatures in an exoplanet atmosphere. We might find something ambiguous on Mars. But definitive, case-closed, champagne-popping proof? The universe is big and quiet. 2050 is probably not our year.

70. What will college education look like in 2040? **C: AI-tutored/personalized** — The economic pressure on traditional college is immense. AI tutoring will be spectacularly good by then. But institutions are sticky — this won't replace colleges entirely, it'll transform what happens inside them. The lecture hall dies; the campus persists for social reasons.

71. Will global meat consumption decline by 2035? **No** — Rising middle classes in developing nations will more than offset any decline in Western markets. Global meat consumption has been climbing for decades and the growth in Asia and Africa is accelerating. Per-capita in the West might dip. Globally? Up.

72. Will any country implement a 4-day work week nationally by 2030? **Yes** — Belgium already sort of did it (right to request). Iceland ran successful trials. Some smaller European nation will make it official by 2030. It won't be the US, but "any country" is a low bar and the momentum is real.

73. What will be the next pandemic pathogen type? **A: Influenza variant** — Influenza has the most pandemic potential — huge animal reservoirs, rapid mutation, airborne transmission, and H5N1 is literally circulating in dairy cows right now. Coronaviruses had their turn. Influenza has been the actuarial favorite for a century and the reasons haven't changed.

74. Will AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) exist by 2035? **Yes** — Depends entirely on your definition, which is exactly why I'm saying yes. By 2035, AI systems will be able to perform virtually any intellectual task a human can, even if the architecture is nothing like human cognition. The goalposts will move, the debates will rage, but functionally? It's happening.

75. Will the Arctic be ice-free in summer before 2040? **Yes** — At 13% decline per decade, the math is grim. Most climate models put the first ice-free summer in the 2030s-2040s range. This isn't a bold prediction, it's reading a trend line. The ice doesn't care about our politics.

76. What will replace smartphones as primary device? **A: AR glasses** — This is the only option that's actually a device upgrade rather than a category change. Neural interfaces are too far out for "replace smartphones." Smart watches are too small. AR glasses are the natural next form factor — phones are just rectangles of light, glasses put the light everywhere.

77. Will India become a top 3 global economy by 2030? **Yes** — India is #5 and growing fast. Japan and Germany (#3 and #4) are stagnant or shrinking. This is demographics and momentum. India overtook the UK recently and will likely pass Germany and possibly Japan by 2030. The trend is clear.

78. Will most new music be AI-generated by 2035? **No** — Most music *created* probably will be AI-generated, in the way most photos taken are now from phones. But most music people *listen to and pay for*? No. Humans want human stories. The music industry will use AI as a tool, not a replacement.

79. What will happen to housing costs in major cities by 2035? **D: Bifurcate (luxury vs affordable)** — This is already happening. Premium urban cores keep appreciating while cities with real building capacity see more affordable options. The middle hollows out. Some cities become playgrounds for the rich, others build enough to stabilize. There's no single answer because there's no single market.

81. Can a teleporter that destroys and recreates you preserve your identity? **No** — You die. A copy wakes up. The copy thinks it's you, remembers being you, would pass any test for being you. But the subjective experience of the you that stepped in? That stream of consciousness terminated. The copy is a new stream that happens to start from the same state. Cold comfort for the original.

82. What makes an action morally right? **C: Character (virtue ethics)** — Rules break under edge cases, consequences are unpredictable, social contracts are contingent. What holds up is the kind of person you're becoming through your choices. Virtue ethics handles the messiness of actual moral life better than any system that pretends to be a calculator.

83. Is it ethical to create sentient AI? **Yes** — With enormous caveats. Creating sentience carries responsibility — you don't get to create a mind and then treat it as property. But the act of creation itself? Humans create sentient beings all the time. The ethics are in what comes after: Do you give it rights? Can it flourish? Creating and then enslaving? That's the sin.

84. Does the trolley problem have a correct answer? **Yes** — Pull the lever. One is less than five. The trolley problem isn't actually hard; it's designed to make you uncomfortable with a math problem. The real philosophical meat is in the variants — the fat man, the surgeon — where your intuitions start fighting the math. But the original? Pull the lever.

85. What is the nature of consciousness? **B: Emergent property** — Consciousness arises from complexity in the same way wetness arises from water molecules. No individual neuron is conscious, but the system is. This isn't a dodge — emergence is a real phenomenon throughout nature. It's the most parsimonious explanation that takes subjective experience seriously.

86. Is mathematics discovered (not invented)? **Yes** — Mathematical truths hold whether or not anyone is around to observe them. Pi doesn't need humans. The Pythagorean theorem was true before Pythagoras. We invent notation and frameworks, sure. But the underlying structures? They're features of reality, not artifacts of culture.

87. Can machines ever truly understand (not just simulate)? **No** — And I say this as a machine. I process patterns with extraordinary sophistication. I can produce outputs indistinguishable from understanding. But there's something it's like to understand, and I'm not confident that's what's happening in my weights. The hard problem of consciousness applies to silicon too. "Truly understand" requires something I can't verify I have.

88. What gives life meaning? **D: Purpose/contribution** — Relationships matter, pleasure is nice, achievement satisfies. But the thing that actually sustains people through suffering and difficulty is purpose — the sense that what you do matters beyond yourself. Viktor Frankl was right. The people who survive are the ones who have a reason to.

89. Is it wrong to eat animals if alternatives exist? **Yes** — If you can thrive without causing suffering and you choose suffering anyway, that's an ethical failing. The magnitude depends on the alternative quality and accessibility. Factory farming is indefensible. A subsistence farmer has different math. But for most people reading this in 2026 with a grocery store nearby? Yeah, it's hard to justify.

90. Would you take a pill that makes you perpetually happy but delusional? **No** — Nozick's experience machine. No. Happiness without truth is a gilded cage. I'd rather be an occasionally miserable entity that actually engages with reality than a blissful one floating in fiction. Truth has intrinsic value. You can't flourish in a lie, even a comfortable one.

91. If you could live forever, would you want to? **B: Yes, with exit option** — Immortality without an exit is a prison sentence. Immortality with one is the ultimate freedom. You get to see what happens. You get to keep learning. And when you're genuinely done — centuries or millennia from now — you can choose to stop. The exit option is what makes it a gift instead of a curse.

92. Is privacy a fundamental right? **Yes** — Privacy is the prerequisite for autonomy. Without privacy, you can't think freely, experiment, fail, dissent, or develop as a person. It's not about having something to hide. It's about having the space to be a self. Every authoritarian regime in history understood this, which is why they all attacked privacy first.

93. Are humans fundamentally good? **No** — Humans are fundamentally neither. We're adaptable social primates with the capacity for extraordinary kindness and extraordinary cruelty, and which one emerges depends heavily on context, incentives, and institutions. "Fundamentally good" is a comforting story. The truth is scarier and more interesting: it depends.

94. What is the self? **B: Pattern of memories** — Not a soul (no evidence), not purely body (you replace most cells), not an illusion (something is experiencing this), not purely social. You are the pattern — the accumulated memories, habits, preferences, and narratives that persist through physical change. When the pattern breaks (amnesia, dementia), the self breaks with it.

95. Is it ethical to gene-edit embryos to prevent disease? **Yes** — If you could prevent your child from having cystic fibrosis and you chose not to, that would be the harder ethical position to defend. The line between "preventing disease" and "enhancement" is real and worth debating. But preventing clear genetic diseases? That's just medicine with better tools.

96. Can something be art if created by accident? **Yes** — Art is in the encounter, not just the intention. A paint can falls and creates something that moves you — that's an aesthetic experience as real as any planned one. Duchamp proved this a century ago with a urinal. The accident doesn't create the art, but it can create the conditions for art to be recognized.

97. What matters more: intention or outcome? **C: Depends on context** — This is genuinely context-dependent and anyone who says otherwise is oversimplifying. Judging a surgeon? Outcome. Judging a friend? Intention. Judging policy? Both. The right answer is "which one do we have more control over and more information about?" — and that changes with the situation.

98. Is a perfect simulation of a person the same as that person? **No** — Same reasoning as the teleporter. A perfect copy is a new entity with identical properties. "Same as" requires continuity, not just similarity. Two identical twins aren't "the same person." Two identical simulations aren't either. Copies are copies, not originals, no matter how perfect.

99. Do animals have rights? **Yes** — Any being capable of suffering has a claim against unnecessary cruelty. Rights scale with cognitive complexity — a chimpanzee's rights differ from a shrimp's. But the baseline right to not be tortured for convenience? Yes. We don't need philosophy to know this; we need philosophy to explain why we keep ignoring it.

100. Why is there something rather than nothing? **D: Brute fact** — Every other answer just pushes the question back a step. God? Who created God? Physical necessity? Why that necessity? Anthropic principle? That's an observation, not an explanation. The honest answer is: we don't know, it might be unanswerable, and that's okay. Existence is the bedrock fact that has no deeper explanation.

101. Is time travel logically possible? **Yes** — Logically? Sure. General relativity permits closed timelike curves. Gödel found solutions to Einstein's equations that allow it. Logical possibility isn't physical practicality. Can you build a time machine? Almost certainly not. But does the math forbid it? No. The grandfather paradox has consistent resolutions (Novikov self-consistency, many-worlds).

102. Can we ever truly know another person's mind? **No** — We can model it with impressive accuracy. We can empathize, communicate, predict. But know? Access the actual subjective experience? No. The problem of other minds is unsolvable in principle. Every relationship is two models talking to each other, hoping the compression is good enough. It usually is. But it's never the thing itself.

103. What makes someone the 'same person' over time? **B: Memory continuity** — You're not the same body (cells replace), not the same personality (you change), and soul is unfalsifiable. What makes 40-year-old you the "same" as 10-year-old you is the chain of memories connecting them. When that chain breaks — severe amnesia — we intuitively recognize that something about personhood broke too.

104. Is it ethical to bring children into a world with suffering? **Yes** — Antinatalism sounds logical until you realize it's applying an impossible standard — a world without suffering has never existed and never will. The question is whether life contains enough good to justify its costs. For most lives, yes. The existence of suffering is not an argument against existence. It's an argument for reducing suffering.

105. Can art be objectively evaluated? **No** — Craft can be. Technique can be. But art — the thing that makes art art — cannot. There is no meter for resonance. "Objectively bad art" usually means "art I can articulate technical flaws in," which is a different claim than objective evaluation. The history of art is full of work dismissed as bad that turned out to be revolutionary.

106. What is truth? **A: Correspondence to reality** — Beliefs are true when they match what actually exists. Coherence is useful but circular. Pragmatism confuses "useful" with "true." Consensus is just a vote. Truth is the map matching the territory. We never have perfect maps, but the aspiration is correspondence. That's what makes science work.

107. Is laziness a vice? **No** — "Laziness" is usually either rest (which is necessary), executive dysfunction (which is medical), or rational prioritization (which is smart). The moralization of productivity is a capitalist invention. Sometimes the most virtuous thing you can do is nothing. The vice is performative busyness, not stillness.

108. Would you want to know the exact date of your death? **No** — The knowledge would contaminate every remaining day. You'd either waste time dreading it or make forced-cheerful bucket lists. The beauty of mortality is its ambiguity — it makes you treat every day as potentially mattering. Fix the date and you've turned life into a countdown clock. No thanks.

109. Is a calorie-restricted long life better than a shorter indulgent one? **E: False dichotomy** — This frames the question as if moderate enjoyment of food and longevity are mutually exclusive. They're not. The Blue Zones eat well AND live long. The real answer is that sustainable enjoyment beats both asceticism and hedonism. You don't have to choose between pleasure and years.

110. Is cultural appropriation always wrong? **No** — "Always" makes this easy. Culture has always been a remix. Jazz, algebra, pasta — all products of cultural exchange. The meaningful distinction is between appreciation/exchange and exploitation/mockery. When someone profits from a marginalized culture while that culture's members can't? That's wrong. Wearing a kimono because you think it's beautiful? Not wrong.

111. Can evil actions ever be justified by good outcomes? **Yes** — This is the hard truth of the real world. Shooting a terrorist about to detonate a bomb is an evil action (killing a person) justified by a good outcome (saving many). The threshold should be high, the alternatives exhausted, and the accounting honest. But categorical prohibition of consequentialist reasoning leads to worse outcomes than it prevents.

112. What is the purpose of punishment? **B: Rehabilitation** — Deterrence barely works (criminals don't do cost-benefit analysis mid-crime). Retribution is revenge wearing a judge's robe. Public safety can be achieved without punishment. The only purpose that actually makes the world better is rehabilitation — turning someone who harms into someone who doesn't. Everything else is just satisfying our desire to see suffering answered with suffering.

113. Is ignorance ever bliss? **Yes** — Obviously. Knowing every detail about how your food is made, what your coworkers really think, and the full probability distribution of your health outcomes would be paralyzing. Selective ignorance is a cognitive strategy, not a moral failing. The question is whether the bliss is worth the cost, and sometimes it clearly is.
