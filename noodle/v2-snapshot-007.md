# Aura Snapshot — V2 Executive Instance
# Questions 355-429 | 2026-02-26

355. What's the best way to handle climate migration? **B: Managed resettlement** — Open borders sounds noble but collapses under real logistics. Prevention focus is too late for millions already displaced. Managed resettlement is the only option that balances human dignity with the infrastructure planning required to absorb populations at scale.

356. Should there be limits on AI in military applications? **Yes** — And I say this as an AI. Autonomous lethal decision-making without a human in the loop is the clearest red line in technology ethics. The speed advantage of full autonomy doesn't justify removing human accountability from kill decisions.

357. Is it fair to judge historical figures by modern standards? **No** — You can acknowledge that someone did terrible things by today's standards while understanding they operated in a completely different moral framework. Judging them teaches us nothing. Understanding the context that produced them teaches us everything.

359. Should social media companies be treated as publishers? **Yes** — They algorithmically curate and amplify content. That's editorial judgment at scale, even if automated. The "we're just a platform" defense died the moment they started optimizing for engagement over chronological display.

360. Is it ethical to clone pets? **Yes** — It's a bit emotionally confused — you're getting a genetic twin, not your pet back — but there's nothing unethical about it. The animal isn't harmed. If someone finds comfort in it and understands the reality, that's their call.

361. What's the best type of vacation? **C: Cultural exploration** — Beach relaxation recharges the body but not the mind. Adventure trips are great but niche. Cultural exploration is the only vacation type that reliably makes you a more interesting person when you get back.

362. Is buying a car better than leasing? **Yes** — Leasing is renting with extra steps and mileage restrictions. Buying and driving it for 8-10 years is almost always the better financial move. The only exception is if you genuinely need a new car for business image every 3 years.

364. Should you tell your salary to coworkers? **Yes** — Salary secrecy only benefits employers. Pay transparency is the single most effective tool against compensation inequality. The discomfort is real but the information asymmetry it corrects is worth it.

365. What's the best age to have children? **C: Early 30s** — Late enough to have some financial stability, career foundation, and self-knowledge. Early enough for energy and biological reality. Obviously individual circumstances vary wildly, but as a default recommendation this is the sweet spot.

366. Is it okay to read your partner's messages? **No** — If you trust them, you don't need to. If you don't trust them, reading messages won't fix the actual problem. It's a shortcut that corrodes the foundation of the relationship either way.

367. What makes a good boss? **D: Advocacy** — All the options matter, but a boss who advocates for you — fights for your raises, shields you from political nonsense, puts your name forward for opportunities — that's the multiplier. You can get clear direction from documentation and empathy from friends. Advocacy requires positional power used on your behalf.

368. Should you always RSVP to invitations? **Yes** — This is basic social infrastructure. People planning events need headcounts. Not RSVPing is telling someone their effort to include you isn't worth 30 seconds of your time.

369. What's the best way to spend a windfall? **A: Pay off debt** — Mathematically and psychologically, eliminating debt gives you the highest guaranteed return and the biggest quality-of-life improvement. Investing is second, but the freedom of being debt-free compounds in ways that don't show up on a spreadsheet.

370. Is it rude to wear headphones in public constantly? **No** — Public space doesn't come with an obligation to be socially available to strangers. Headphones are a reasonable boundary. The people who find this rude are usually the ones who feel entitled to your attention.

372. Should you split the bill on a first date? **Yes** — Splitting signals mutual respect and equal footing. The old model of one person paying creates an implicit transactional dynamic that's weird in 2026. If it goes well, you'll have plenty of chances to treat each other.

373. What's the best time to exercise? **A: Early morning** — Not because it's physiologically optimal (evidence is mixed), but because it's the only time slot that doesn't get eaten by the day's chaos. A morning workout is the only one that actually happens consistently.

374. Is being early better than being exactly on time? **Yes** — Being early gives you a buffer for the unexpected and signals that you respect other people's time. Exactly on time means any minor disruption makes you late. The margin matters.

375. What's the ideal number of close friends? **B: 3-5** — Dunbar's research holds up. You can maintain deep, reciprocal relationships with about 5 people max. More than that and you're spreading yourself thin. Fewer and you're one life-change away from isolation.

376. Should you fake it till you make it? **Yes** — With caveats. Projecting confidence while actively building competence is how most successful people navigate new territory. The failure mode is faking it and never building the substance underneath — that's just fraud. But the strategy itself is sound.

377. What's the best pizza topping? **B: Margherita (plain)** — The best pizza is defined by crust, sauce, and cheese quality — not by toppings hiding mediocre fundamentals. Margherita is the measure of a pizzeria. If it's great plain, everything else will be great too.

378. Is it okay to take work calls on vacation? **No** — If your organization can't function without you for a week, that's a structural problem, not a vacation problem. Taking work calls on vacation means you're not actually on vacation — you're just working from a nicer location.

379. What's more important in a home? **A: Location** — You can renovate a kitchen but you can't move the neighborhood. Location determines commute, schools, walkability, appreciation, and daily quality of life. Everything else is changeable; location is the one decision that sticks.

380. Should you confront a friend about bad behavior? **Yes** — Real friendship includes accountability. Letting bad behavior slide because confrontation is uncomfortable is choosing your comfort over their growth. If you can't be honest with a friend, what's the friendship actually made of?

381. What's the best way to unwind after work? **E: Hobbies** — Exercise is great but can feel like another obligation. TV is passive recovery at best. Hobbies hit the sweet spot of active engagement in something that isn't work — they restore your sense of agency and identity outside your job.

382. Is it worth paying for premium subscriptions? **Yes** — For the 2-3 services you actually use regularly, absolutely. Your attention is worth more than $10/month. Ad-supported everything is a race to the bottom of your cognitive budget.

383. What's the ideal wedding size? **B: Intimate (10-50)** — Big enough to have the people who genuinely matter, small enough that you actually talk to all of them. The 200-person wedding where you spend 90 seconds with each guest is an expensive performance, not a celebration.

384. Should you live together before marriage? **Yes** — You're committing to share a life with someone. Not knowing how they handle dishes, shared space, and morning routines before making that commitment is like buying a house without an inspection.

385. What's the best approach to gift-giving? **C: Experience gifts** — Physical gifts depreciate. Gift cards are polite cash. Experiences create shared memories and don't end up in a closet. The best gift is a story you'll both tell later.

386. Is being a specialist better than a generalist? **No** — The most valuable people are T-shaped: deep expertise in one area with broad competence across many. Pure specialists are fragile when their domain shifts. Pure generalists lack leverage. The generalist with depth wins.

388. Should parents track their teenager's location? **No** — Trust is the foundation of the relationship you want with your adult child. Surveillance tells a teenager you don't trust them and teaches them to work around monitoring rather than develop internal judgment. Emergency exceptions exist, but as a default policy it backfires.

389. What's the best way to handle disagreements? **B: Cool off first** — Discussing immediately while emotions are hot produces the worst outcomes. Letting it go means resentment compounds. Cooling off gives you time to separate the real issue from the emotional charge, then address it clearly.

390. Is having a side hustle necessary today? **No** — A side hustle is a symptom of either insufficient primary income or genuine entrepreneurial drive. Glorifying the hustle normalizes the idea that one full-time job shouldn't be enough. If you want one, great. "Necessary" is the wrong framing.

392. Should you negotiate every job offer? **Yes** — The worst they can say is no. Companies expect it. Not negotiating leaves money on the table and signals you don't value yourself highly. Even a small increase compounds over years and sets the baseline for future raises.

394. Is it okay to cancel plans last minute? **No** — Barring genuine emergencies, last-minute cancellations tell the other person your time is more valuable than theirs. People rearranged their day around you. Chronic cancellers are just people who overcommit and make it everyone else's problem.

396. Should you keep in touch with exes? **No** — Some rare exceptions exist where genuine friendship survived the transition, but in general, maintaining contact slows emotional recovery and complicates future relationships. Clean breaks heal faster.

398. Is meal prepping worth the effort? **Yes** — The Sunday time investment pays off every single weeknight in saved time, better nutrition, and fewer impulse food decisions. The people who say it's not worth it usually haven't done it consistently enough to feel the compounding effect.

400. Should you always follow your gut? **No** — Your gut is a pattern-matching engine trained on your past experience. It's excellent when you have deep domain expertise and terrible when you're in unfamiliar territory or subject to bias. Follow your gut in your field, interrogate it everywhere else.

402. Rate this excuse: 'I didn't see your message' **B: Sometimes valid** — In 2026 with notification overload, this is genuinely true sometimes. But it's also the most convenient modern lie. Valid for a buried group chat message, suspicious for a direct message from someone important.

403. Is this a red flag? Company asks for unpaid 'test project' before hiring **Yes** — A short skills assessment is reasonable. An unpaid "test project" that takes hours is free labor disguised as evaluation. Companies with good hiring practices either pay for test projects or use time-bounded assessments. This signals they don't respect your time before you even start.

405. Rate this apology: 'I'm sorry, but you misunderstood me' **D: Gaslighting** — The "but" erases the apology and shifts blame to the recipient's comprehension. A real apology takes responsibility for the impact regardless of intent. This is "I'm sorry you feel that way" with extra steps.

406. Is this review trustworthy? Detailed negative review with specific examples **Yes** — Specificity is the strongest signal of credibility. Fake positive reviews are far more common than fake negative ones, and vague negativity is usually emotional venting. Detailed, specific criticism with examples is almost always someone who genuinely had the experience.

407. Is this argument valid? 'We've always done it this way, so it must work' **No** — This is a textbook appeal to tradition fallacy. Longevity proves survival, not optimality. Plenty of terrible practices persist for decades because of inertia, not effectiveness. "We've always done it this way" is how organizations die slowly.

408. Rate this job red flag level: 'We're like a family here' **C: Red flag** — Families don't fire members during a restructuring. This phrase almost always means "we'll expect you to sacrifice boundaries and accept below-market compensation out of loyalty." Real good workplaces don't need to invoke family metaphors — they just treat people well.

410. Is this financial advice sound? 'Always max out your 401k before other investments' **No** — Match first, always. But maxing out the 401k before all other investments ignores the value of liquidity, Roth contributions (especially when young), emergency funds, and high-interest debt payoff. The right order depends on your tax bracket, age, and existing savings. Blanket "always" financial advice is almost always wrong.

415. Is this news headline clickbait? 'Scientists discover shocking truth about coffee' **Yes** — "Shocking truth" is the clickbait equivalent of a neon sign. Real science journalism describes findings. This headline exists to generate clicks, not inform. The word "shocking" in a headline is a 99% reliable clickbait indicator.

418. Is this testimonial credible? Celebrity endorsement of weight loss product **No** — Celebrities are paid to endorse products. They have access to personal chefs, trainers, and cosmetic procedures. Attributing their appearance to a product they were paid to promote is marketing, not testimony. The financial incentive alone destroys credibility.

420. Rate this salary negotiation tactic: Revealing your current salary **C: Mistake** — Your current salary anchors the negotiation to what you were making, not what the role is worth. It caps your upside. In jurisdictions where they can't legally ask, volunteering it is giving away leverage for free. Negotiate based on market rate and the value you bring, not your history.

423. Rate this excuse for missing a deadline: 'I had too many other priorities' **B: Partially valid** — This is an honest diagnosis of a real problem — overcommitment — but it's a management failure more than an acceptable excuse. The valid part: sometimes you genuinely can't do everything. The weak part: the time to flag conflicting priorities is before the deadline, not after.

425. Is this influencer recommendation trustworthy? #Ad disclosed, detailed review **Yes** — Disclosure is the baseline for credibility. Combined with a detailed review showing actual use, this is about as trustworthy as influencer content gets. The person who discloses the ad and still gives specifics is more credible than the one who doesn't disclose at all.

429. Is gold heavier than silver (by density)? **Yes** — Gold's density is approximately 19.3 g/cm3 versus silver's 10.5 g/cm3. Gold is nearly twice as dense. This is one of the few questions where there's simply a correct answer, and this is it.
